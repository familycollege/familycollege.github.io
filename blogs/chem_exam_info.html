How to Prepare for the Chemistry Olympiad if You Really Want to Achieve the National Level Part II

Chemistry Olympiad Exam Info

The U.S. National Chemistry Olympiad (USNCO) consists of three rounds, with one local exam and two national exams. The local exam is usually held in March. The first National Exam (round 2) and the final National Exam take place in April and June, respectively.

Exam Structure:

1. Local Exam (Round 1):

  • 60 multiple-choice questions in 110 minutes.
  • Over 10,000 students participate annually. The top two scoring students from each high school in this round qualify for the National Exam. Approximately 1,000 students take the second round of the National Exam.

2. National Exam (Round 2):

  • Part 1 (Multiple Choice): 60 questions in 90 minutes, covering a range of chemistry topics like thermodynamics, kinetics, equilibrium, and organic chemistry.
  • Part 2 (Free Response): 8 written questions requiring detailed calculations and explanations. This section lasts 105 minutes.
  • Part 3 (Lab Practical): 2 laboratory tasks to be completed in 90 minutes. Previous tasks include chromatography, titration, and qualitative analysis.

The average national score varies year by year, but for Part 1, students generally need to score around 30-35 out of 60 to be competitive.

After the second round of the exam, the top 20 students are invited to the Study Camp, where further testing and lab work determine the final team of four students who represent the U.S. at the International Chemistry Olympiad.

3. The Final National Exam (Round 3):

The final National Exam of the U.S. National Chemistry Olympiad (USNCO) consists of three parts, and it is much more competitive than the local exam. The National Exam is taken by around 1,000 students, with the top performers advancing through the stages.

  • Part 1: Multiple Choice
    • 60 questions in 90 minutes.
    • These questions cover a wide range of chemistry topics, including stoichiometry, thermodynamics, kinetics, equilibrium, and organic chemistry.
  • Part 2: Free Response
    • 8 written questions in 105 minutes.
    • Students must provide detailed explanations and calculations. Topics include organic chemistry, equilibrium, bonding theories, and others.
  • Part 3: Lab Practical
    • 90 minutes to complete two laboratory tasks.
    • Tasks often involve techniques like titration, chromatography, and qualitative analysis.

Only the top 4 students are invited to attend the International Chemistry Olympiad.
